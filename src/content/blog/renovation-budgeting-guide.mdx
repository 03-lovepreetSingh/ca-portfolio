Renovation budgets have a well-earned reputation for spiralling out of control. The cause is rarely dishonest contractors — it is most often a mismatch between what homeowners expect and what a realistic project actually costs.

This guide gives you a framework for building a number you can trust.

## Start With a Scope Document, Not a Number

Before you ask anyone for a quote, write down what you want done in plain language. "Kitchen renovation" is not a scope. "Remove wall between kitchen and dining room, replace cabinets, install quartz countertops, new flooring throughout, upgrade electrical panel to accommodate induction range" is a scope.

The more specific your scope, the more comparable your quotes will be. Vague scopes invite vague quotes — and vague quotes are where budget surprises hide.

## The Real Cost Categories

Most residential renovation budgets break down like this:

- **Labour**: 40–50 % of total project cost for most trades work.
- **Materials**: 30–40 %, depending on the finish level you choose.
- **Permits and inspections**: 2–5 %, non-negotiable for structural, electrical or plumbing work.
- **Design and engineering**: 5–10 % if drawings are required (often mandatory for structural changes).
- **Project management**: included in most general contractor quotes; expect 10–15 % of trade costs.

These numbers shift significantly based on your city. Toronto and Mississauga labour rates run 15–20 % higher than the provincial average.

## Build In a Contingency Fund — Always

> "We budget 15 % contingency for renovations in older homes and 10 % for newer builds. In 12 years of contracting, we have never had a client wish they had saved less in contingency."
> — Northline project manager

Once walls are open, surprises are almost inevitable in homes built before 1990: knob-and-tube wiring, asbestos insulation, water damage, inadequate structural headers. A 10–15 % contingency is not pessimism — it is sound financial planning.

## How to Read a Contractor Quote

A trustworthy quote will itemise:

1. A clear description of the work included (and excluded).
2. Labour costs broken out from material costs.
3. Allowances — placeholder amounts for materials you have not selected yet (fixtures, tile, hardware). Know that low allowances are a classic way to make a quote look attractive on paper.
4. A change-order clause that spells out how scope additions are priced.
5. Payment milestones tied to completion stages, not calendar dates.

If a quote is a single lump sum with no breakdown, ask for the detail. A contractor who cannot or will not provide it is a red flag.

## The Cheapest Quote Is Rarely the Best Value

Three quotes for the same scope routinely come back with a 30–40 % spread. The cheapest quote often reflects one or more of the following:

- Lower-grade materials substituted without disclosure.
- Subcontractors who are not licensed or insured.
- Unrealistic timelines that will result in overtime charges.
- A scope that quietly excludes something your other quotes include.

Compare line by line, not total to total.

## Phasing Works — With a Plan

If the full scope exceeds your budget today, phasing is a legitimate strategy — but only with a master plan in hand. Renovating the kitchen now and the adjacent hallway "later" without coordinating the two means you will likely redo work or pay for premium access twice.

Ask your contractor to scope both phases together so the sequencing, rough-ins and finishes are planned as a whole.
